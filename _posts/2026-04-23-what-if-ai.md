title: "What if AI was the best thing to ever happen to software quality?"
date: 2026-04-23
---

Imagine you're part of a team of ten software developers, and your manager told you that ten new developers will join the team next week. 
What do you think that team change will do the reliability of your team's services and the team's developer productivity? 

My guess: the reliability and productivity would drop through the floor in a matter of weeks. A few months later, most of your old team mates will have left due to operational load, and having to spend most of their day reviewing pull-requests.


What if we flipped the script? What if your manager had instead asked "I got ten open headcount. But how can we make such a huge change to the team ends up improving both productivity and reliability?"

Most engineers I know would be overjoyed at the opportunity to reduce incident risk and pay down tech debt. This is exactly the type of work that ends up on the backburner until it can no longer be ignored.


The challenges of adopting AI are similar to the challenges of massively expanding a team. Most teams I know would need to invest in architecture, testing, early incident detection, automatic incident resolution, and CI/CD reliability. This is exactly the kind of work that's worthy of the term 'software engineering'.

Effective AI adoption can be a massive win/win for engineering and management. So how come many tech companies are heading for the lose/lose of mandated AI use and it's inevitable side-effects?
