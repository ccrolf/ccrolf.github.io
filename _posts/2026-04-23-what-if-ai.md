If you want your AI adoption to fail, here's a simple recipe: focus on token use, obsess over which model to use, and ignore the fundamentals of developer productivity.

# Thought experiment 
Imagine you're part of a team of ten software developers. One day your manager tells you that ten more developers are joining next week. What will happen to reliability and productivity?

My guess: both crater within weeks. A few months later, half of your old teammates have left. Burned out by spending their days reviewing pull-requests and their nights resolving incidents.

What if your manager had instead asked "We have ten open headcount. But how can we double the team without sacrificing productivity or reliability?"

Most engineers I know would be overjoyed at that question. Reducing incident risk and paying down tech debt is the type of work that gets left on the back burner until it can no longer be ignored.


# The investment nobody wants to make
No team can double productivity overnight, regardless of whether it's with people or AI. Teams need to invest in architecture improvements, test quality, CI/CD reliability, early incident detection, and faster incident resolution. This is exactly the kind of work that's worthy of the term 'software engineering'.

Done right, AI adoption is a win/win for engineering and management. 

I've led developer productivity, including AI adoption, across 50 engineers for the past two years. So far, we've increased developer productivity by 100% and developer satisfaction by 50%.

But if you're still bent on failing at AI adoption, here's an even simpler recipe: stop asking, stop listening, and just mandate.
