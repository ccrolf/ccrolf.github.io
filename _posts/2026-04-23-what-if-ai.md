If you want your AI adoption to fail, here's a simple recipe: treat it as a technology problem, roll it out to the whole company, and measure success by adoption rate.

We've all seen this happen enough times to know that it works.


# Thought experiment 
Imagine your team of ten will double next week. No ramp time, no process changes, just ten more engineers pushing code.

What happens to reliability and productivity?

Both crater. The engineers who know which capabilities are held together with hope and duct tape are suddenly spending their days reviewing code written by people who don't understand the business. Incidents take longer to resolve because the people who normally jump in are underwater. Three months later, those same engineers, the ones you can't afford to lose are updating their CVs. 

Now here's the question that should have come first: "What would we need to invest in before we make this change?"

Most engineers never get asked that question, but most engineers know the answer: tests that catch regressions before customers, CI/CD pipelines that don't require babysitting, incident detection that names likely root causes and mitigations, and architecture that limits failures rather than propagates them. These are the investments that make scale safe. It's also the work that engineers have wanted to prioritise for years. The work that doesn't matter until it's the only thing that matters.

Done right, AI adoption is the strongest argument engineering has ever had to finally make these investments. The teams getting this right aren't the ones who moved the fastest. They're the ones who reduced operational load and created space for the work that retains good people. 

The ones who optimized for speed and skipped the investments are now quietly dealing with the consequences, while their best engineers are looking for the exits.


# An even simpler recipe
But if my simple recipe feels like too much work, here's a shorter one: stop asking, stop listening, and just mandate.

That one works every time for every problem.
